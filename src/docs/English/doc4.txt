Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890)
The Prophet of Expressionist Art
Vincent Willem van Gogh is one of the most celebrated and emotionally compelling figures in the history of Western art. Born on 30 March 1853 in Zundert, in the southern Netherlands, van Gogh pursued a career as an artist for only the final decade of his short life, yet in that period he produced a body of work of extraordinary intensity, originality, and lasting influence. His paintings, characterised by their bold colours, expressive brushwork, and emotional urgency, anticipated the Expressionist movement that would profoundly shape twentieth-century art.
Van Gogh's path to painting was circuitous. He worked successively as an art dealer, a teacher, and a lay preacher before committing himself to art in his late twenties. His early works, produced largely in the Netherlands, are characterised by sombre earth tones and a preoccupation with the lives of peasants and rural labourers. The Potato Eaters (1885), depicting a family of peasants sharing a meagre meal by lamplight, exemplifies this early style and reveals his deep moral sympathy for those living in poverty.
The decisive transformation of van Gogh's art occurred following his move to Paris in 1886, where his encounter with Impressionist and Post-Impressionist painting — particularly the work of Monet, Pissarro, and Seurat — introduced him to a radically different understanding of colour and light. His palette brightened dramatically, and his brushwork became increasingly energetic and expressive. Subsequently moving to Arles in the south of France in 1888, he entered the most productive period of his career, producing such celebrated works as The Sunflowers, The Bedroom, and Café Terrace at Night in a sustained burst of creative energy.
Among his most iconic works is The Starry Night (1889), painted during his voluntary stay at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence. The painting depicts a nocturnal landscape of swirling stars and a luminous crescent moon above a sleeping village, rendered with a dynamic, almost cosmic energy that transforms mere visual observation into an expression of profound inner experience. It has become one of the most recognised and reproduced artworks in the world.
Van Gogh's relationship with Paul Gauguin, with whom he briefly shared a house in Arles, ended in the infamous incident in December 1888 when, following a violent quarrel, van Gogh severed part of his own left ear. This episode has become inseparable from his public image, though it has also, unfortunately, tended to overshadow the genuine intellectual seriousness and artistic rigour that characterise his work and his voluminous correspondence.
His most important achievement lies in his radical reconception of the purpose of colour and form in painting. For van Gogh, colour was not merely a descriptive tool but a direct vehicle for emotional and spiritual expression — an insight that would prove foundational for Expressionism, Fauvism, and much of the abstract art of the twentieth century.
Van Gogh died on 29 July 1890 in Auvers-sur-Oise, France, two days after sustaining a gunshot wound. He sold only one painting during his lifetime. Today, his works command among the highest prices ever achieved at auction, and his influence on the trajectory of modern art is incalculable.
