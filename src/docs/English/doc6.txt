 Michelangelo (1475–1564)
The Divine Craftsman of the High Renaissance
Michelangelo di Lodovico Buonarroti Simoni — known simply as Michelangelo — is universally acknowledged as one of the greatest artists in the history of Western civilization. Born on 6 March 1475 in Caprese, a small town in the Tuscan Apennines, Michelangelo excelled as a sculptor, painter, architect, and poet, achieving in each discipline a level of mastery that his contemporaries recognized as transcending ordinary human capacity. Indeed, his fellow Italians accorded him the title Il Divino — "the Divine One" — a sobriquet that captures the quasi-supernatural admiration his work inspired.
Michelangelo's primary artistic identity was that of a sculptor. He believed that the ideal form existed already within the marble block, waiting to be liberated by the artist's chisel — a philosophy that he expressed in his celebrated remark that sculpture is the art of taking away superfluous material. His marble sculptures possess a physical and emotional intensity that has never been surpassed. The Pietà (1498–1499), completed when Michelangelo was just twenty-three years old, depicts the Virgin Mary cradling the body of Christ with a tenderness and anatomical precision that astonished his contemporaries. Today it remains one of the most celebrated sculptures in the world, housed in St. Peter's Basilica in Rome.
His David (1501–1504), a monumental marble statue standing over five meters tall, depicts the biblical hero in the tense moment before his confrontation with Goliath. The figure's idealized anatomy, psychological alertness, and commanding physical presence established it as the supreme embodiment of Renaissance humanist ideals — the dignity, beauty, and potential of the human form as a reflection of divine creation.
Michelangelo's achievement as a painter, though secondary in his own estimation to his work as a sculptor, produced one of the most celebrated decorative schemes in Western art. The ceiling of the Sistine Chapel in the Vatican, painted between 1508 and 1512 under the patronage of Pope Julius II, comprises a vast narrative programme drawn from the Book of Genesis, culminating in the iconic scene of The Creation of Adam, in which God's outstretched finger approaches Adam's with an intimacy and power that has made the image one of the most recognizable in human cultural history.
His later fresco The Last Judgement, painted on the altar wall of the Sistine Chapel between 1536 and 1541, presents a dramatically different vision — darker, more turbulent, and charged with Counter-Reformation anxiety about sin, judgement, and human frailty.
As an architect, Michelangelo's design for the dome of St. Peter's Basilica — though not completed until after his death — remains one of the most influential architectural achievements of the Renaissance, establishing a model for domed structures that was imitated across Europe for centuries.
Michelangelo died on 18 February 1564 in Rome, just weeks before his eighty-ninth birthday. His legacy encompasses not merely a body of masterworks of staggering ambition and quality, but an enduring demonstration of the power of art to render visible the deepest aspirations of the human spirit.
